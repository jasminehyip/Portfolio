Project: Rebrand

PRODUCT REDESIGN

A fictional redesign for a brand done as a student project, with the goal of honing my design and marketing skills. For this product, I decided to do a redesign, adapting the marketing and communication methods for Canadian society.

PRODUCT

おいしい野菜

“Delicious Vegetables” is a Japanese Dietary Supplement in the form of a jelly stick, and is well known product among Japan's society as a health product. It is manufactured by FINE Japan Co. Ltd., and the product is under their line of supplement jellies called “Supplement Jellies”.

Sold as an affordable commodity, it is packed with the nutritional benefits of one meal's worth of vegetables (120g), meeting the guidelines set by the Ministry of Health in Japan. 22 different kinds of vegetables and 45 vegetable extracts, with only 1g of sugar per stick; they are diet-friendly, and the individually packed sticks make them easy to carry around and eat at any time.

idea.

RECOLOURS

For the colour theme, I used the existing colour palette, except slightly muted the colours, maintaining the original theme. As the goal of the rebrand was to make the messaging and packaging appropriate for the western market, I chose not to stray away from the current brand colours.

PACKAGING

Fully English and French translated packaging, carefully following Canadian Food Inspection Agency (CFIA) guideline, with clear communication of product goals and intended use for non-Japanese audiences.

**NOTE Due to limitations, French translations are not perfect and were translated online.

Why

Rebrand?

1. JAPANESE-ONLY PACKAGING

T&T Supermarket, one of the largest Asian supermarket chains in Canada, is a seller of this health supplement, "Oishii Yasai". However, despite the product being sold internationally in western countries like Canada, there is zero English-packaging. This hinders the products sellability and success in a foreign market; especially as it is a unique product — a supplement in the form of a jelly-stick — it has the potential to gain a competitive advantage against other sellers of vitamin supplements, if marketed properly.

2. LAYOUT & COMMUNICATION

For western standards, there is too much information and text on the packaging of the box. Even if you cannot read or understand Japanese, something that is clear is that there are inconsistencies with colours, organization of information (mainly on the sides and back); with current trends and tastes, there is room for improvement to communicate key aspects of the product in a clear and concise way. In Japan, this may be passable as it is a well-known product within their society, however the product recognition is not strong enough in the West, and poor design only decreases its chance of success.

3. PRICE & BRAND IMAGE

The product is currently positioned as an everyday commodity, but this works in Japan because the price is about ¥1160 (~$11 CAD). Due to import costs, this product is sold in Canada at $33.99 +tax, meaning that selling it as an everyday commodity does not make sense in this market. The brand image needs to align with how it's realistically being sold.

REFLECTION

This was my first time doing a rebranding project, and I'm really proud of the packaging design and the English-version of the brand name I was able to come up with. I learned about how the Big Idea of a project is critical for strategic decision making when it comes to the design, such as colour, fonts and messaging. The next time I do a product rebrand, I'd like to explore bolder packaging and branding design layouts and colours that cater to current modern design trends.
